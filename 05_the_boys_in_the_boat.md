# The Boys in the Boat, by Daniel James Brown

## Bottom line

Read this if you want accessible narrative nonfiction about teamwork, poverty, amateur sport, and the 1936 Berlin Olympics. Skip it if you dislike inspirational sports history or already know you prefer more analytical history.

## What the book is about

The book tells the true story of the University of Washington men's eight-oar crew that won gold at the 1936 Olympics in Berlin. Brown builds the story around Joe Rantz, a working-class student who had been abandoned by his family and had to earn his way through school. The larger cast includes his teammates, coach Al Ulbrickson, and boatbuilder George Pocock, whose reflections on rowing give the book much of its emotional vocabulary.

The rowing story is intercut with Depression-era hardship, University of Washington rivalry with elite eastern schools, the craft of shell-building, the physics and psychology of crew synchronization, and Nazi Germany's staging of the 1936 Games. It is a classic underdog structure: lower-income western students learn to move as one body and defeat better-funded rivals before facing Italy and Germany in Berlin.

## Why a16z recommends it

Jason Saltzman recommends it as an Olympic story and as a time capsule of the amateur era. His deeper reason is the idea of "swing": the rare condition in which individual effort disappears into perfectly coordinated group motion. That is the business and team-building hook. The book is not just "sports are inspiring"; it is about how a group becomes more than the sum of strong individuals.

## What you would get from reading it

- A concrete language for teamwork, rhythm, trust, and coordination.
- A view of Depression-era grit without turning it into abstraction.
- A satisfying narrative arc that is easier to read than most books on this list.
- An account of amateur sport before sponsorship, media machinery, and professionalized youth pipelines.
- A useful contrast between individual biography and collective achievement.

## Caveats

The book is emotionally direct and conventionally told. That is a feature if you want a page-turner; a limitation if you want a more critical or revisionist account of the 1936 Olympics. The 2023 film foregrounds Joe Rantz, but Brown has said the original book was meant to honor the whole boat.
